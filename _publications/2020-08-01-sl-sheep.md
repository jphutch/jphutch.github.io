In this paper, we analyze demand for sheep coat color among sheep-owning households in Sierra Leone. In West Africa, sheep play an important role in religious ceremonies which some studies claim makes white-coated sheep more valuable. We test this hypothesis by designing a discrete choice experiment which uses scienti􀏐ic illustrations to vary the weight and coat color of a representative West African Dwarf sheep, the most common breed in Sierra Leone. We 􀏐ind that respondents were willing to pay about twice as much for a sheep with a pure white coat compared to the more common black and white sheep. Sheep with only a few black spots are valued the same as sheep with far less white in their coats, underscoring the importance of sheep being pure white. Our results suggest that cultural preferences for coat color could be an important factor in whether households adopt new sheep breeds.
